Overview

"What's Next?" is a browser-based interactive ESL module designed for intermediate-level English Language Learners at the secondary level. The lesson builds the language of future thinking — goal-setting, sequencing, expressing hopes and intentions — through scaffolded activities, contextual vocabulary, and a culminating reflection that asks students to articulate a next step of their own.

The question "What's next?" is both linguistically rich and genuinely meaningful to adolescent learners navigating school, family, and identity in a second language. The lesson uses that authenticity as its engine — language acquisition follows more readily when the task asks for something real.

The Challenge

For intermediate ELLs at WIDA Levels 3–4, the instructional gap is precise: students have enough English to navigate daily life but not yet enough to express complex, forward-looking thought with confidence. The language of planning — conditionals, future modals, sequencing phrases, expressed intention — is exactly where that gap shows up most visibly. It surfaces in college advisement conversations, in goal-setting check-ins with counselors, in writing assignments that ask "where do you see yourself?"

Existing materials for this level tend toward either grammar drills stripped of context or open-ended prompts that presuppose language students haven't been taught. The challenge was to occupy the productive space between: structured enough to give students real language, open enough to ask for real thinking.

Approach

The lesson opens with the question directly — not as a grammar prompt but as a genuine invitation. Students encounter the language of future-thinking through realistic scenarios before they're asked to produce it themselves. Vocabulary and sentence frames are embedded in the activity flow, appearing where they're needed rather than front-loaded in a list.

Each activity follows the same cognitive arc: encounter the language in context, process it actively, produce something with it. Scaffolds gradually release across the lesson — visual supports and partially completed frames appear early and taper toward the end as students build confidence. The final screen holds a single open field: one real next step, in the student's own words. The design keeps the interface quiet so attention stays on the language. No unnecessary animation, no competing visual information — the lesson gets out of its own way.
