Unit 1

The Developing Brain & Screens

The brain your child has today is still under construction — and its environment is the contractor.
1

Before we talk about screen time rules or app restrictions, we need to talk about the brain — because without understanding how a child's brain actually develops, none of the other advice makes sense. The developing brain is not a smaller adult brain. It is a fundamentally different organ at a different stage of construction — and it is exquisitely sensitive to its environment.

The human brain is not fully developed until approximately age 25. From birth through adolescence, the brain undergoes dramatic structural changes: forming synaptic connections at extraordinary speed in early childhood, then pruning and refining those connections throughout adolescence. The prefrontal cortex — responsible for impulse control, decision-making, risk assessment, and long-term thinking — is the last region to mature. This means children and teenagers are literally not neurologically equipped to self-regulate technology use the way adults can. The environment children are immersed in during these years shapes the brain's wiring in lasting ways. The process of synaptic pruning — where the brain eliminates unused connections — means that what children practice and experience during this window literally shapes which neural pathways are preserved.

Research Says:

A landmark 2018 NIH study (the ABCD Study — Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development) found that children who spent more than 2 hours per day on screens scored lower on thinking and language tests. Brain scans showed differences in cortical thickness in children with higher screen time — changes in the very structure of the developing brain.

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter associated with reward system activation, motivation, and learning. Every notification, like, and autoplay video is engineered to trigger a small dopamine release — enough to reinforce the behavior and make the child want more. This is not accidental. The designers of these platforms have studied behavioral design and apply it deliberately. For a child whose dopamine system is still developing, this creates a particularly powerful pull. Repeated dopamine spikes from fast-reward digital experiences can recalibrate the brain's baseline expectations for stimulation threshold — making slower rewards like reading, conversation, or creative play feel less satisfying by comparison. The reinforcement schedule used by social media platforms — variable rewards delivered unpredictably — is the same pattern that produces compulsive slot machine behavior.

Research Says:

Former Google design ethicist Tristan Harris has described these techniques as "a race to the bottom of the brain stem" — deliberately exploiting the most primitive reward circuits.

Sustained attention — the ability to focus on a single demanding task for an extended period — is a learned cognitive skill that the developing brain must practice to build. Research from Microsoft and multiple academic sources has documented declining attention spans in populations with high digital media use. More significantly, the constant task-switching demanded by notifications, feeds, and multi-tab browsing trains the brain toward shallow, fragmented attention — the opposite of what academic learning, creative work, and complex problem-solving require. Deep focus is not a trait — it is a skill that must be developed through practice, and it is precisely this practice that digital distraction interrupts. Dr. Gloria Mark at UC Irvine found it takes an average of 23 minutes to fully regain deep focus after an interruption. For children checking phones repeatedly throughout a homework session, meaningful deep learning may not be occurring at all.

The brain consolidates memory, clears metabolic waste, and regulates emotion during sleep — particularly during deep slow-wave and REM sleep cycles. Blue light emitted by screens suppresses melatonin production, delaying sleep onset and reducing sleep quality. Beyond blue light, the emotional stimulation from social media — comparison, conflict, excitement — elevates cortisol and keeps the nervous system aroused long after the device is put down. The process of sleep consolidation — where the brain encodes new information into long-term memory — is disrupted when sleep is shortened or fragmented. The American Academy of Pediatrics and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine both recommend no screens for at least one hour before bedtime. Emotional regulation — a core skill adolescents need — is significantly impaired by sleep deprivation, creating a feedback loop where screen use disrupts sleep, poor sleep impairs regulation, and poor regulation drives more screen use as a coping mechanism.

Research Says:

A 2023 study in JAMA Pediatrics found that adolescents who used screens after 9 PM had significantly higher rates of depressive symptoms and anxiety — independent of total screen time.

Unit 2

The Early Years (Ages 0–5)

The first five years of life are the most critical window of brain development in a human lifespan. Language acquisition, attachment, emotional regulation, and foundational cognitive wiring are all being built during this period — and they are built through one primary mechanism: human interaction. Understanding what screens replace during this window is more important than any screen time number.

Between birth and age three, the brain forms approximately one million new neural connections per second. These connections are built through serve-and-return interaction — the back-and-forth of eye contact, facial expression, vocalization, and responsive touch between a child and a caregiver. Language acquisition is not learned from video or audio alone at this age — it requires a live, contingent interaction with a responsive human. The famous video deficit effect, documented extensively in research, shows that children under 3 learn significantly less from screens — even educational content — than from identical live human interaction. This is not about screen time being harmful per se — it is about screens being an inefficient substitute for what the developing brain actually needs. The implication is not guilt — it is clarity about what the window requires.

Research Says:

A 2016 study in JAMA Pediatrics found that each additional 30 minutes of daily handheld screen time in toddlers was associated with a 49% increased risk of expressive speech delay.

The AAP guidelines recommend: no screen time for children under 18–24 months (except video chat); limited, high-quality programming only for ages 2–5, co-viewing with a parent; and consistent limits with screen-free time for ages 6 and up. These are not arbitrary rules. They are calibrated to developmental windows — periods where displacement of human interaction carries measurable cost. Video chat (FaceTime, Zoom) is specifically exempted because it maintains the contingent human interaction the brain needs. Passive consumption — YouTube, streaming — does not. The co-viewing recommendation is significant: a parent who watches and talks about content with a child transforms a passive experience into an interactive one — partially compensating for the deficit, though not eliminating it.

Research on technology's impact on early childhood increasingly focuses not only on children's direct screen use — but on the distracted parent. A 2014 study published in Pediatrics observed playground interactions and found that parents absorbed in smartphones were less responsive to their children's bids for connection, and children escalated risky behavior to recapture parental responsiveness. The still face experiment — a landmark developmental psychology study by Dr. Ed Tronick — demonstrates that even brief, unresponsive parental behavior causes measurable distress in infants. The smartphone is, in effect, a sophisticated still face machine for the modern parent. Understanding this is not about blame — it is about awareness. The phone creates a pattern of attachment disruption that is invisible in the moment but cumulative in effect. Parents who understand this dynamic are better equipped to choose when to be fully present.

Research Says:

A 2023 report from the Surgeon General cited evidence that parental smartphone use during key interaction windows can disrupt the serve-and-return process critical to infant brain development.

Vocabulary, grammar, and communication skills develop through rich, varied, back-and-forth conversation — what researchers call language nutrition. Children who are talked to more, read to more, and engaged conversationally develop measurably larger vocabularies and stronger literacy foundations. Background television — even when a child is not watching — reduces adult-child conversational turns significantly. Studies show that for every hour of background television, children hear approximately 770 fewer words from caregivers. The implications for language development compound over years — a child who hears significantly fewer words each day during the critical language window enters school with a measurably smaller vocabulary, and that gap is difficult to close. The solution is not complicated: talk, read, sing, and narrate. These are the inputs the developing language system is built for.

Unit 3

The Elementary Years (Ages 6–11)

Elementary-age children are building identity, social skills, and the cognitive habits they'll carry into adulthood. This is the window when children learn how to learn — and it's increasingly being competed for by the gaming industry, YouTube's algorithm, and a device in every pocket. This is not about banning screens — it's about understanding what unstructured, creative, and social offline time is building, and what its absence costs.

Gaming is not uniformly harmful — cooperative play, problem-solving games, and age-appropriate titles have documented cognitive benefits including spatial reasoning, planning, and social bonding. The problems emerge with specific game types and use patterns: games engineered with variable reward schedules (like loot boxes), online multiplayer games with social pressure to stay connected, and gaming as a primary mode of stress regulation. The World Health Organization formally recognized Gaming Disorder — their WHO classification — as a diagnosable condition in 2019. Risk factors include more than 2–3 hours of daily gaming, gaming as the primary social outlet, and emotional dysregulation when gaming is limited. The distinction between gaming as one activity among many versus gaming as the organizing center of a child's social and emotional life is critical.

Research Says:

A 2022 study in JAMA Network Open found that children who played video games for 3+ hours per day performed significantly better on cognitive tasks related to memory and impulse control — but also showed increased risk of depression when gaming was used to cope with negative emotions.

YouTube's recommendation algorithm is optimized for one thing: watch time. It does not optimize for age-appropriateness, accuracy, or developmental benefit. For children, this creates a well-documented phenomenon: content progressively escalation — intensifying in stimulation and extremity — because extreme content holds attention longer. A child searching for Minecraft videos can, within minutes, arrive at content involving violence, conspiracy, or adult themes — without clicking a single inappropriate link. YouTube Kids exists, but its filter failure is widely documented and criticized. Autoplay is the most dangerous feature for children: it removes the friction of active choice and replaces it with passive drift. The solution is not just filters — it is the development of a child's own capacity to recognize when content has shifted and to choose to stop. That capacity must be explicitly taught.

The research on unstructured play, child-directed and free, is unambiguous and extensive. It builds executive function, emotional regulation, creativity, social negotiation, frustration tolerance, and resilience. Dr. Stuart Brown, founder of the National Institute for Play, describes play as "the most significant factor in determining success and happiness across a lifespan." As screen time has increased over the past two decades, outdoor free play has declined by approximately 50% in the United States. Children today spend an average of 4–7 minutes per day in unstructured outdoor play. The displacement effect is real: time spent on screens is time not spent building these foundational skills. None of these skills — frustration tolerance, conflict resolution, creative problem-solving — are built by watching. They are built by doing, failing, and trying again.

Research Says:

A 2023 analysis by the American Psychological Association linked the decline in free play directly to rising rates of anxiety and depression in children aged 6–12, calling it "one of the most significant and under-discussed public health shifts of our generation."

Unit 4

The Middle School Danger Zone (Ages 11–14)

No previous generation of adolescents has faced what today's 11-year-olds face on day one of social media.
4

If there is a single window in childhood that demands the most parental vigilance around technology, it is ages 11 to 14. This is the period when social media typically enters a child's life — and it arrives precisely when the adolescent brain is most identity-vulnerable, most socially sensitive, and least equipped for the psychological demands these platforms impose.

Puberty triggers a dramatic restructuring of the brain's reward and social circuitry. The sensitivity of the social pain network spikes — rejection, exclusion, and humiliation register more intensely during early adolescence than at any other developmental window. Peer evaluation becomes the brain's primary preoccupation. Social comparison — always present — becomes hyperactivated. Into this neurological environment, social media introduces a quantified, public, permanent social ledger: likes, follower counts, comment threads, and comparison at a scale no previous generation has faced. The adolescent brain's reward circuitry is particularly sensitive to peer approval — which means social media metrics become neurologically significant in a way they simply are not for adults. Identity formation — the central developmental task of adolescence — is happening in real time, in public, on a platform designed to maximize engagement at the cost of wellbeing.

The correlation between social media adoption and adolescent mental health decline is among the most extensively studied questions in contemporary developmental psychology. Dr. Jean Twenge (San Diego State University) documented sharp increases in depression, anxiety, loneliness, and suicidal ideation beginning precisely in 2012 — the year smartphone ownership became ubiquitous among adolescents. Dr. Jonathan Haidt (NYU Stern) and colleagues have assembled the most comprehensive review of the literature to date, concluding that social media is a primary causal contributor to the adolescent mental health crisis — particularly for girls. Instagram has been shown in internal Facebook research (leaked in 2021) to worsen body image in 32% of teen girls who already felt bad about their bodies. The evidence has moved beyond correlation — the timing, the demographics, and the dose-response relationship all point toward causation.

Research Says:

The U.S. Surgeon General issued an advisory in 2023 stating that social media use poses a "profound risk of harm to the mental health and well-being of children and adolescents." He called for warning labels comparable to those on alcohol and tobacco.

Cyberbullying differs from traditional bullying in critical ways: it is available 24/7 with no geographic boundary, it can be anonymity-protected, it is often permanence-enforced and publicly visible, and it can spread instantly to an entire peer group. The Cyberbullying Research Center estimates that approximately 27% of students experience cyberbullying. For LGBTQ+ youth, the rates are significantly higher. The psychological impact is severe: cyberbullying victims show significantly elevated rates of depression, anxiety, self-harm, and suicidal ideation compared to both non-bullied peers and victims of traditional in-person bullying. The 24/7 nature of cyberbullying is particularly important — traditional bullying ended when the school day did. Cyberbullying follows the child home, into their bedroom, and into the night. There is no refuge.

Social media feeds present a curated reality — a filtered, performed presentation of life, not life itself. For adolescents still forming their identity and self-worth, constant comparison to these curated presentations is psychologically corrosive. Studies using passive use (scrolling without posting) consistently show the strongest association with depression and low self-esteem. Active use (posting, connecting, direct messaging) shows weaker and sometimes positive associations. The mechanism is clear: passive consumption of others' highlight reels triggers upward social comparison — a known driver of dissatisfaction and diminished self-worth across all age groups, but especially in adolescents. Teaching children to recognize that what they are seeing is a performance — not a life — is one of the most important conversations a parent can have about social media.

Unit 5

AI in School: What Parents Need to Know

Artificial intelligence tools — particularly large language models like ChatGPT, Google Gemini, and Claude — entered classrooms and homework routines almost overnight, with almost no preparation for students, parents, or most educators. Understanding what these tools are, how children are using them, and what cognitive skills their misuse undermines is now a baseline parenting competency.

Large language model systems are sophisticated statistical systems trained on vast text data. They generate plausible-sounding text by statistical prediction — predicting what words and sentences are likely to follow each other — they do not understand, reason, or retrieve verified facts. They hallucinate — confidently stating false information with no signal of uncertainty. For children using AI to complete homework, these AI limitations are critical: AI-generated responses may be fluent, well-structured, and wrong. Children who do not have sufficient subject knowledge for critical evaluation of AI output are particularly vulnerable to accepting and submitting inaccurate information. The confidence of the output is not correlated with its accuracy — and children must be explicitly taught this. An AI that sounds authoritative is not an authority.

A 2023 Stanford study found that over 60% of high school students had used AI to complete or assist with assignments. A Common Sense Media survey found that 50% of teens used AI for homework weekly. The range of use spans the spectrum: from legitimate use as a thinking partner or editor, to full cognitive outsourcing of writing, research, and problem sets. The most concerning pattern is not the tool use itself — it is the displacement of the cognitive struggle that produces learning process. Writing a draft, making mistakes, and revising is how writing skill is built. Having an AI produce the draft removes the very process that creates competence building. The issue is not academic integrity in the narrow sense — it is about what the student is actually developing, and what they are trading away for a cleaner submission. AI use in school that bypasses struggle is not a shortcut to learning — it is a bypass around it.

Research Says:

A 2024 MIT study found that students who used AI to write essays showed short-term performance gains but significantly weaker writing ability when tested independently — suggesting AI use was displacing rather than augmenting skill development.

The most important thing education can build in the age of AI is the capacity for critical thinking: to evaluate information, construct arguments, recognize logical reasoning failures, and think independently. These skills are built through practice — through struggling with hard problems, writing and rewriting, debating and being wrong. Children who use AI as a shortcut are not just avoiding work — they are avoiding the practice that builds the cognitive architecture AI cannot replicate. The capacity for evaluation — to read a claim and assess its credibility, to encounter an argument and identify its weaknesses — is precisely what makes a human being more than a prompt-input machine. Parents need to understand this not to police AI use, but to have honest conversations with their children about what they are trading away when they outsource thinking.

The goal is not to ban AI use — it is to develop AI literacy. Children should understand what AI is, how it works at a basic level, and when its tool use helps vs. hurts their own development. Helpful questions parents can ask: "Did you use AI on this? What did it help with? What did you do yourself?" This transparency conversation normalizes honesty and opens dialogue rather than driving use underground. Framing AI as a tool — like a calculator — helps: useful for certain tasks, but a substitute that creates dependency when used to bypass foundational skill-building. School policy on AI use is evolving rapidly; parents should ask their child's school directly what its current policy is and how it is being enforced. The conversation about academic integrity in the AI era is one that parents, educators, and students need to have openly — before the habit of outsourcing becomes default.

Unit 6

Online Safety Fundamentals

Online safety is often reduced to "don't talk to strangers" — a framework that was never adequate and is even less so now. Today's risks are more varied, more sophisticated, and more likely to arrive through a child's own social network than through a stranger. This unit covers what parents actually need to know.

Every search, post, like, location tag, and account registration creates a permanent digital footprint. Children routinely share their school name, neighborhood, sports team, and daily schedule through public social media without understanding that this information can be aggregated by anyone to locate them physically. Photos contain EXIF metadata including GPS coordinates unless stripped. Platform privacy settings are deliberately confusing and default to maximum data collection. A 2022 study found that the average child's data is sold to a median of 72 third-party data brokers before age 13. Teaching children about their digital footprint is not about paranoia — it is about informed decision-making. Every piece of shared information is a choice — and children deserve to make that choice with full understanding of its implications.

Research Says:

The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) prohibits platforms from collecting data on children under 13 — but platform age verification relies almost entirely on self-reporting, and 40% of children under 13 have active social media accounts.

The NCMEC (National Center for Missing and Exploited Children) received 32 million reports of suspected child sexual exploitation online in 2022 — up from 18.4 million in 2018. Online grooming — the process by which an adult builds trust building with a child over time to facilitate exploitation — typically begins not with explicit requests but with friendship, attention, flattery, and the establishment of secrets. Grooming increasingly occurs through gaming platforms and direct messaging, not just social media. Children are often not aware they are being groomed — and the shame associated with the process means they are unlikely to self-report without active, non-judgmental parental communication. The most protective factor is not surveillance — it is a child's certainty that they can tell a trusted adult without fear of losing their device or being blamed.

Research from the Journal of Adolescent Health found that approximately 15% of adolescents have sent a sexual image of themselves, and 27% have received one. In most US states, sending or possessing sexual images of minors — even between minors — constitutes a criminal offense under child pornography statutes. These legal consequences are not hypothetical — adolescents have been prosecuted and placed on sex offender registries for sexting. Non-consensual sharing of intimate images — image-based abuse — is a documented source of severe psychological harm in adolescent victims, including depression, PTSD, and suicidal ideation. Children need to understand that an image sent privately can become permanent and public instantly, and that no relationship — romantic or otherwise — guarantees an image will remain private. The phrase "once it's sent, it's theirs" is a framework every adolescent needs to hear before they face the pressure to send.

Children encounter misinformation online constantly — from health misinformation to historical distortion to political extremism. The algorithmic amplification architecture of social media and video platforms is specifically optimized to push content that generates emotional engagement — and misinformation, outrage, and extremism generate more engagement than factual, moderate content. Research on radicalization pathways documents a consistent pattern: gradual escalation from mainstream content toward increasingly extreme viewpoints, driven by platform recommendations rather than active seeking. Media literacy — the ability to evaluate sources, recognize manipulation, and think critically about online content — is now a foundational safety skill. It is not enough to teach children what to avoid; they must be taught how to evaluate what they encounter. The question "how do we know this is true?" is one of the most important habits a parent can build in a child. Source evaluation is not an academic skill — it is a safety skill.

Unit 7

Building Healthy Tech Habits

The goal is not to raise children who are afraid of technology or who lack digital skills. The goal is to raise children with agency over their attention, clarity about technology's effects, and a rich offline life that screens supplement rather than replace. This unit is about practical, evidence-informed strategies that work.

Research on parental mediation of technology consistently shows that reactive restriction (banning, confiscating) is less effective than proactive engagement — talking with children about technology, co-using it, setting boundaries together, and building shared family agreements. The American Academy of Pediatrics Family Media Plan tool allows families to create individualized agreements covering screen-free zones, screen-free times, content standards, and device management. The most important elements: screens out of bedrooms at night, no devices during meals, and consistent modeling by parents of the behavior expected from children. Proactive mediation works because it builds children's own capacity to self-regulate — rather than simply creating an external constraint that disappears when the parent is not present. Co-viewing and discussion of media content is consistently associated with better outcomes than either unrestricted access or pure prohibition.

Given the weight of evidence on screens and adolescent sleep disruption, the single most evidence-supported intervention available to parents is: charge all devices outside the bedroom ban, overnight, every night, starting now. This one change has been associated in multiple studies with improved sleep duration, reduced depression symptoms, better academic performance, and reduced morning irritability. The device charging protocol — devices plugged in outside the bedroom before the child goes to sleep — addresses both blue light exposure and the social anxiety of overnight notifications. Sleep hygiene is not a soft intervention. It is among the most evidence-based recommendations in adolescent health. The resistance children and adolescents show to this change is itself a signal — and a reason, not an exception. The strength of the resistance is proportional to the depth of the habit.

Research Says:

A 2019 JAMA Pediatrics study of over 4,500 adolescents found that bedroom smartphone access at night was independently associated with depression and anxiety — even controlling for total screen time.

Children who feel they can come to parents without judgment when something goes wrong online are significantly safer than children who feel they must hide their online lives. Building this requires consistent, non-reactive communication about technology before problems arise. Effective approaches: ask open questions about what your child is doing online rather than interrogating; name that you know online life can be complicated; establish in advance that if something bad happens online, your first response will be to help, not punish. Psychological safety — the child's certainty that they can disclose without catastrophic consequence — is the most protective factor in online safety research. When children know the phone will not be taken away as punishment for reporting a problem, they are far more likely to report problems. Building trust requires consistency over time — it cannot be established in a single conversation, but it can be destroyed by a single reactive response.

Ages 0–5: no independent screen use; all screen time parent-supervised and co-viewed. Ages 6–9: limited daily time (1–1.5 hours), no social media, no unsupervised YouTube, no devices in bedrooms. Ages 10–12: introduce media literacy conversations; devices stay in common areas; no personal social media accounts; gaming with time limits and game-type awareness. Ages 13–15: social media may be introduced with transparent monitoring, ongoing conversation, and clear family agreements; privacy is earned through demonstrated responsibility. Ages 16+: increasing autonomy with continued open communication. These are age-appropriate frameworks, not rigid rules — individual children vary, and the relationship is always the most important factor. The developmental framework is a scaffold: the goal is a child who can eventually manage technology well on their own, not one who is perpetually managed by external controls.

Unit 8

Resources & Next Steps

You've made it through the full course — and you now know more than most parents, most teachers, and most school administrators about these issues. What matters now is not perfection — it's forward motion. Below are the most credible, practical resources available on every topic covered in this course.

Common Sense Media (commonsensemedia.org) — the most comprehensive independent resource for reviews of apps, games, shows, and research on children and technology. Center on Media and Child Health at Boston Children's Hospital (cmch.tv) — research-based, clinician-developed resources. NCMEC — National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (missingkids.org) — CyberTipline, NetSmartz online safety curriculum for kids. The Social Dilemma / Center for Humane Technology (humanetech.com) — advocacy and resources for reforming platform design. Fairplay (fairplayforkids.org) — campaigns against predatory marketing and data collection targeting children. Each of these organizations maintains freely available resources for parents — guides, research summaries, conversation starters, and policy updates. Media literacy resources from Common Sense Media are particularly useful for parents who want ready-made tools to use with children of all ages.

The Anxious Generation — Jonathan Haidt (2024): the most comprehensive examination of social media's role in the adolescent mental health crisis. iGen — Jean Twenge (2017): data-driven analysis of how smartphone culture has reshaped Generation Z. Stolen Focus — Johann Hari (2022): how attention has been deliberately fragmented and what to do about it. The Art of Screen Time — Anya Kamenetz (2018): a balanced, evidence-based guide for parents navigating technology decisions. Glow Kids — Nicholas Kardaras (2016): a neuroscientist's argument that screen technology functions similarly to addiction in the developing brain. All of these are research-backed and written for a general audience — they are genuinely accessible to parents without a background in neuroscience or developmental psychology, and each offers a meaningfully different perspective and emphasis.

AAP Family Media Plan (healthychildren.org/MediaUsePlan) — build a customized family agreement in minutes. Google Family Link — parental controls and screen time management for Android and Chromebook. Apple Screen Time — built-in iOS parental controls for iPhone and iPad. Circle Home Plus — device-level content filtering and screen time management for all home network devices. Bark — monitoring tools that alerts parents to potential safety concerns without reading full message content — balances safety with adolescent privacy. The Bark model is particularly worth noting: rather than full message surveillance, it uses pattern detection to flag concerning content — preserving more of the adolescent's reasonable expectation of privacy while still providing safety oversight. This approach tends to preserve the relationship better than full monitoring, which adolescents often experience as a fundamental breach of trust. Circle offers network-level controls that apply to all devices in the home.

CyberTipline (cybertipline.org): report suspected child exploitation online — operated by the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children, available 24/7. Crisis Text Line: text HOME to 741741 for free, 24/7 crisis counseling — accessible to adolescents who may not be ready to speak out loud. National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988 — call or text. SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (mental health and substance use). Internet Crimes Against Children Task Force (icactaskforce.org): law enforcement resources for online crimes against children. These crisis resources and emergency reporting tools should be saved, known, and discussed with children before they are needed. Knowing these resources exist — and knowing a child knows they exist — is part of building the safety net that makes online risk manageable.
