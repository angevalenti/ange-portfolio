The Do's

Habits that build a strong professional reputation

✓
1
Use a clear subject line

Your subject line is a preview of your professionalism. "Question about Thursday's meeting" beats "hey" every time.

Email
✓
2
Proofread before sending

Read it once for content, once for tone, once for typos. If it's important, read it out loud.

Writing
✓
3
Respond within 24 hours

Even if you don't have the answer yet, acknowledge the message. "Got it — I'll follow up by Friday" goes a long way.

Responsiveness
✓
4
Match the platform to the message

Quick update? Slack. Detailed request? Email. Sensitive feedback? Face-to-face or video call. Never fire someone over text.

Channels
✓
5
Start meetings with context

Don't assume everyone remembers last week's discussion. A 30-second recap saves 10 minutes of confusion.

Meetings
✓
6
Ask before you assume

If a message feels off, ask for clarification instead of reacting. Tone is hard to read in text.

The Don'ts

Common mistakes that quietly damage your reputation

✓
7
Don't use ALL CAPS

It reads as shouting. If you need emphasis, use bold or italics sparingly.

Tone
✓
8
Don't reply-all unless necessary

Before hitting reply-all, ask: does everyone on this thread actually need to see my response?

Email
✓
9
Don't send angry messages

Write it, save it as a draft, walk away for 10 minutes. If you still want to send it after cooling down, revise the tone first.

Conflict
✓
10
Don't ghost professional contacts

If you can't make a meeting or need to decline, say so. Silence isn't professional — it's confusing.

Reliability
✓
11
Don't overshare in work channels

Slack and Teams are work tools, not group chats. Keep personal updates for personal conversations.

Boundaries
✓
12
Don't skip the sign-off

End every professional email with a closing: "Best," "Thanks," "Looking forward to hearing from you." It signals you're done talking.

Email
💡 Pro Tip

The best communicators aren't the ones who write the most — they're the ones who make every word count. When in doubt, shorter is almost always better.
