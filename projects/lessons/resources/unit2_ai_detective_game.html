Unit 2: AI Tools & You

AI vs. Human Writing
Detective Game

Can you tell the difference? Test your skills.

AI-generated writing is everywhere — essays, emails, articles, social media posts. Learning to spot it is becoming one of the most important critical thinking skills of 2026. In this activity, you'll read 6 passages and decide: was it written by a human or by AI?

1
Passage 1 of 6
"In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, it's important to note that artificial intelligence has fundamentally transformed the way we interact with technology. These advancements offer unprecedented opportunities for growth and innovation across multiple sectors."
🤖 AI Written
The tell: Vague filler phrases ("it's important to note," "unprecedented opportunities"), no specific examples, unnaturally smooth sentence flow. No human voice, no specifics, no stakes.
2
Passage 2 of 6
"I didn't even realize I was using AI until my friend pointed out that the email I sent sounded nothing like me. It was too perfect, too polished. I'd let Gmail's smart compose write half of it."
✍️ Human Written
The tell: Personal anecdote, specific detail (Gmail smart compose), natural and self-aware voice, emotional undercurrent. The self-contradiction ("I didn't realize") is a human move — AI doesn't second-guess itself.
3
Passage 3 of 6
"The impact of social media on adolescent mental health has been a subject of considerable debate among researchers and healthcare professionals. Studies suggest that excessive screen time may correlate with increased rates of anxiety and depression."
🤖 AI Written
The tell: Academic hedging without citations ("studies suggest," "may correlate"), no named researchers, overly balanced framing. A real journalist or student would either name a study or share a personal angle. This says nothing specific about anything.
4
Passage 4 of 6
"My little sister is 13 and she already has three finsta accounts. THREE. I asked her why and she said 'one for friends, one for aesthetics, one for venting.' That's a lot of identities to manage before you even have a driver's license."
✍️ Human Written
The tell: Voice, humor, real scenario, direct quotes, the capitalized "THREE" for emphasis. The dry punchline at the end ("before you even have a driver's license") is distinctly human wit. AI doesn't use emphasis for comic effect.
5
Passage 5 of 6
"When crafting a professional email, it is essential to include a clear subject line, maintain a respectful tone, and proofread carefully before sending. Following these best practices will help you communicate effectively in any workplace setting."
🤖 AI Written
The tell: Generic advice, no personality, list-like structure disguised as a sentence. "It is essential to..." is a classic AI phrase. No example, no mistake, no personal experience — just a bland summary of things anyone could Google.
6
Passage 6 of 6
"The worst email I ever sent was to my boss at my summer job. I accidentally wrote 'Hey girlie' instead of 'Good morning' because I was also texting my friend. I wanted to crawl under my desk."
✍️ Human Written
The tell: Embarrassing specificity ("Hey girlie"), emotional reaction ("I wanted to crawl under my desk"), multi-tasking mistake, casual conversational rhythm. AI would never volunteer a story this specific and self-deprecating without being prompted.

⭐ The Golden Rule of AI Detection

If a piece of writing could have been written by anyone about anything, it was probably written by no one. Real writing has fingerprints.
