Overview

Real language.
Not textbook language.

Language Through Film is a self-paced module for secondary English language learners. Instead of vocabulary lists and grammar drills, students work through curated film scenes — picking up idioms, tone, rhythm, and cultural context from how people actually talk. The module is designed for WIDA levels 2–4, with scaffolding that adjusts the difficulty without changing the experience itself.

The Challenge

The gap between
the classroom and the real world.

A lot of ELL materials teach language like it's a set of rules to memorize. Students end up being able to conjugate a verb but not follow a conversation. The real gap is context — understanding why someone said something a certain way, what the tone means, what's being communicated through body language and not just words. Film was the right medium because it delivers all of that at once. But picking the right clips, building the right scaffolding around them, and making interactions that go deeper than 'watch and answer questions' — that took careful planning.

Process Visual
Approach

Three layers
around every clip.

Each module is built around a 60–90 second film clip with three parts: a pre-watch vocabulary primer that introduces key terms with visual support, a guided viewing that pauses at important moments for comprehension checks, and a post-watch reflection that moves from 'what happened' to 'what did it mean.' The scaffolding targets WIDA levels 2–4 — always slightly above where the student is comfortable, but never so far that they're lost. The design is clean and intentional. Nothing about it looks or feels remedial.
